Why AI Demands a New Breed of Leaders
Research Top Pick MIT Sloan Management Review · 2026

MIT Sloan research identifies a distinct leadership profile required for the AI era — one that diverges sharply from traditional executive competencies. The study finds the most critical traits are systems thinking across the enterprise, ability to build trust between human teams and AI, and comfort with genuine ambiguity (not managed ambiguity). The findings are stark: only 11% of organizations report that their leaders are well-prepared to navigate the AI transition, yet 62% plan major AI-driven workforce changes in the next 18 months.

Why it matters: The 11% leadership readiness stat is a headline you can open any talk with — and it directly anchors the case for leadership hiring and development being the first thing AI startups should invest in, not the last.

Companies Are Laying Off Workers Because of AI's Potential — Not Its Performance
Research Harvard Business Review · January 2026

HBR examines the dangerous mismatch driving 2026's AI-related layoffs: organizations are cutting headcount based on what AI might do, not what it demonstrably does today. Gartner data underpins the piece — only 1 in 50 AI investments delivers transformational value, and only 1 in 5 delivers any measurable ROI — yet CEO optimism is driving workforce decisions as if those numbers were inverted. The piece documents several companies that made deep cuts and then quietly backfilled when AI underdelivered.

Why it matters: When your startup clients feel pressure to announce AI-driven reductions because competitors are doing it, this is the data that helps them slow down — and it positions you as the advisor who saved them from a costly mistake.

The "Last Mile" Problem Slowing AI Transformation
Thought Leadership Harvard Business Review · March 2026

The biggest obstacle to enterprise AI isn't the technology — it's the final step of embedding AI into actual human workflows. This piece examines why AI pilots succeed but org-wide rollouts stall: integration complexity, change resistance, and the gap between what AI can do in demos vs. what workers will adopt in practice. The authors propose a "last mile playbook" that treats adoption as a behavior-change problem, not a tooling problem — and identifies the specific roles responsible for bridging that gap.

Why it matters: Every founder building an AI-forward org will hit this wall — the "last mile" framing gives you a concrete lens for advising on AI adoption strategy, and the roles it identifies are ones you can help clients hire for.

AI Unlocks New Power for Employees: Are HR Leaders Ready?
Thought Leadership Josh Bersin / MIT Sloan Management Review · 2026

Writing in MIT Sloan, Josh Bersin argues that AI is creating a new class of "superemployees" — individuals who, with the right tools and training, can perform work that previously required teams. The challenge is that HR systems (L&D, performance management, compensation) are still designed for the old model of individual job performance and are not built to identify, reward, or develop superemployees. Bersin calls for a fundamental redesign of HR's core systems, not incremental AI feature add-ons.

Why it matters: If your startup clients are hiring lean and AI-augmented, their HR infrastructure is almost certainly already outdated — this piece gives you a framework and vocabulary for that specific conversation.

Five Trends in AI and Data Science for 2026
Thought Leadership MIT Sloan Management Review (Davenport & Bean) · 2026

Tom Davenport and Randy Bean's annual forecast identifies five trends: the rise of agentic AI, increased focus on governance, the shift from AI-as-tool to AI-as-colleague, growing demand for human oversight roles, and the emergence of domain-specific fine-tuned models. The piece finds that organizations that moved carefully on AI governance are now pulling measurably ahead of those that moved fast without guardrails — reversing a pattern that held for most of 2024–25.

Why it matters: "Human oversight roles" is a new job family trend you can name and spec for clients right now — and the governance-wins-long-term finding is a direct counterargument to the move-fast-with-AI pressure most founders are under.

Hard Fork Ep. 183: Economist Anton Korinek on "Ghost GDP" and AI's Labor Market Reality
Podcast Hard Fork (NYT) · March 1, 2026

Economist Anton Korinek joins Kevin Roose and Casey Newton to unpack the growing disconnect between AI's rapid technical progress and its still-limited observable economic impact. He introduces the concept of "Ghost GDP" — value being created by AI that existing economic metrics simply can't yet measure — and explains why this gap is causing confusion for policymakers, investors, and workers alike. The episode is surprisingly nuanced on why mass job displacement hasn't materialized on the timeline many predicted.

Why it matters: "Ghost GDP" is a concept that will come up in every serious conversation about AI's economic effects — understanding Korinek's framing will make you significantly sharper in the rooms where startup founders and investors are debating whether to hire or automate.

NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang joins Sarah Guo and Elad Gil for a wide-ranging conversation on the state of AI entering 2026 — including the biggest surprises of 2025, the profitability of inference tokens, and his view on whether AI will create or destroy jobs.
