When Using AI Leads to Brain Fry
Research Top Pick BCG · Mar 5, 2026

BCG's landmark study on AI-related cognitive exhaustion finds that 14% of workers — concentrated in marketing, software development, HR, finance, and IT — report significant mental fatigue from AI tool overuse. Crucially, productivity follows an inverted-U curve: it rises with 1–3 AI tools, then falls sharply at 4 or more. Symptoms include mental fog, difficulty focusing, slower decision-making, and headaches. Two factors materially reduce the effect: manager support (cuts fatigue by 15%) and a genuine work-life balance culture (cuts it by 28%).

Why it matters: For founders aggressively rolling out AI tooling across their teams, this is the first rigorous data showing that more AI tools do not equal more productivity — and that the mitigation is managerial, not technical, which reframes how you help clients think about AI adoption strategy.

The Great Reinvention of Human Resources Has Begun
Thought Leadership Josh Bersin · Jan 2026

Bersin argues 2026 is the year AI stops being a productivity layer and starts fundamentally restructuring HR as a function: AI agents and "Superagents" will automate 30–40% of today's HR roles, and HR Business Partners must evolve from administrators into strategic advisors. He frames this as a shift to "full-stack HR" — AI handles transactional work, humans own judgment, relationships, and change management. The goal is not headcount reduction but unlocking HR's business-value potential through scale and accuracy.

Why it matters: When startup founders ask how to think about building their People function in an AI-first world, this is the clearest current map — and it directly challenges the assumption that HR headcount should scale linearly with company size.

AI Doesn't Reduce Work — It Intensifies It
Research Seen before HBR / UC Berkeley · Feb 2026 First surfaced Mar 11

UC Berkeley's study inside a 200-person tech company documents the "AI workload creep" cycle: AI makes more work feasible, available capacity fills immediately with new tasks, and work bleeds into personal time. Burnout runs at 62% among associates vs. 38% among C-suite — meaning the burden lands hardest on early-career workers. Researchers recommend an intentional "AI practice" with explicit organizational norms about when not to use AI.

Why it's still here: Today's BCG brain fry study is its direct companion — together they form a two-study case that AI-driven burnout is now empirically established, and every founder rolling out AI tooling needs a deliberate adoption framework.

Management as AI Superpower
Thought Leadership Seen before One Useful Thing / Ethan Mollick · 2026 First surfaced Mar 11

Mollick's argument: the highest-leverage skill in the AI era isn't technical fluency — it's the ability to direct, evaluate, and orchestrate AI agents at scale, which maps directly onto traditional management competencies. Managers who can set context, delegate to AI, and quality-check outputs will outperform both non-AI users and solo technical contributors. The thesis is that good management is, structurally, what AI collaboration requires.

Why it's still here: As founders ask what to look for when hiring managers in AI-first orgs, this piece provides the framework — management as the meta-skill, not AI proficiency — and pairs directly with the Bersin HR Reinvention piece above.

AI Has Made Hiring Worse — But It Can Still Help
Recruiting Seen before HBR · Jan 2026 First surfaced Mar 12

HBR's audit of AI in hiring finds that most deployed tools have made screening faster but less accurate — increasing volume while amplifying bias and screening out strong non-standard candidates. The analysis separates where AI genuinely helps (scheduling, outreach, job description optimization) from where it actively hurts (resume screening, structured interview scoring). The piece offers a framework for which hiring stages to automate and which to protect.

Why it's still here: Essential reading before any advisory conversation with founders evaluating AI recruiting tools — gives you the precise taxonomy of where these tools earn their keep vs. where they create legal and quality risk.

Podcast Lenny's Podcast · Phyl Terry ("Never Search Alone")
