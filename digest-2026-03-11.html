Research & Reports
AI Doesn't Reduce Work—It Intensifies It
Research ⭐ Top Pick Harvard Business Review  ·  Feb 2026

UC Berkeley researchers tracked white-collar workers over a full year and found that AI tools significantly increased output and task variety — but also hours worked, cognitive load, and burnout risk. Workers reported AI didn't reduce their workload; it raised the ceiling on how much was expected of them, with nearly 39% reporting productivity gains alongside a parallel rise in always-on pressure.

Why it matters: This is exactly the longitudinal data founders need before scaling AI adoption across their teams — it reframes "efficiency" as "sustainable performance" and gives you a compelling, evidence-backed hook for speaking on AI and workforce culture.

When Using AI Leads to "Brain Fry"
Research Harvard Business Review  ·  Mar 2026

A new HBR study coins "brain fry" for the cognitive overload following sustained high-output AI-assisted work. The research pinpoints context-switching between AI-generated content and original thinking as the primary driver, and offers specific workflow design strategies for managers to mitigate it before it becomes burnout.

Why it matters: A concrete framework for advising early-stage AI startups on workflow design that prevents burnout — one of the most common failure modes you see in fast-scaling AI companies.

New Job Families & Org Design
'Engineer' is so 2025. In AI land, everyone's a 'builder' now
Org Design SF Standard  ·  Mar 5, 2026

Reported from inside AI companies including Walmart and several SF startups, this piece tracks the emergence of "builder" as a new cross-functional role identity — nontechnical employees using AI agents to ship products without writing code. At Walmart, all agent builder roles were filled internally by people who voluntarily retrained, not outside technical hires.

Why it matters: Fresh evidence (6 days ago) that new job families are forming around AI agency rather than engineering credentials — useful when advising founders on early team titles, hiring criteria, and internal mobility programs that turn existing employees into AI builders.

Management as AI Superpower
Leadership One Useful Thing — Ethan Mollick  ·  Jan 2026

Mollick reports from an experimental Wharton course where student teams built functional startups in four days using AI. Management and coordination skills — not technical ability — were the differentiating factor. Teams with strong communicators and decision-makers consistently outperformed technically stronger teams.

Why it matters: A powerful counter-narrative for your talks — the AI era doesn't diminish the value of great leadership hiring, it amplifies it, and this is the data to back that up.
